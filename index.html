03   Case Studies
01
Healthcare · Service Design · Policy

Home Personal Care
for Low-income
Older Adults

Duke-NUS Medical School, commissioned by the Ministry of Health, Singapore. Evaluating the effectiveness of the Care Close to Home (C2H) pilot programme delivering physical, cognitive, social, and personal care to vulnerable seniors in 1–2 room rental flats.

Methods
Survey Semi-structured Interviews Contextual Inquiry Focus Groups
The Challenge

The original research plan relied on self-reporting from elderly service users, who are particularly vulnerable to reporting bias and social desirability bias — risks that could render the evaluation findings unreliable and lead to misguided policy decisions.

My Approach

I identified the structural limitations of the initial design and proposed a methodological redesign — adding focus group interviews with service providers (healthcare aides and social workers) and their managers. This introduced a triangulating perspective, allowing us to cross-validate user self-reports with frontline care team observations, capturing a richer and more accurate picture of unmet needs.

Contextual inquiry in participants' homes revealed the realities of life in 33m² flats — spatial constraints, mobility challenges, and the texture of daily life that no survey could surface. The combination of methods gave us both breadth and depth.

Key Finding

While physical and personal care services were well-received, social care was significantly under-provided. The care teams surfaced a critical gap: seniors' need for social participation — accompanying them to public spaces, facilitating community connection — was not being addressed. This is now understood as "social prescribing."

Impact

Findings and service recommendations from this study were adopted by Singapore's Ministry of Health in the national rollout of the eldercare centre service model from 2021–2024, incorporating befriending, buddying, and community participation services for seniors island-wide.

02
E-commerce · Discovery Research · Mixed Methods

Merchant Archetypes
& Product-Market
Fit at NinjaVan

NinjaVan, Southeast Asia. A two-phase mixed-methods study to rethink how merchants are understood — and to translate those insights directly into product strategy and revenue growth across multiple SEA markets.

Methods
Semi-structured Interviews (n=25) Random + Manual Sampling Log Data Analytics Statistical Modelling
The Challenge

Existing merchant archetypes were defined purely by sales channel and volume — blunt categories that offered no insight into user needs, pain points, or growth trajectories. The product team was targeting the wrong user group for Product X, and there was no data-driven foundation to course-correct.

Phase 1 — Qualitative Discovery

I designed and led 25 semi-structured interviews across Singapore, Malaysia, Thailand, Vietnam, and Indonesia — navigating cross-country recruitment, language barriers, and significant stakeholder management challenges under tight timelines.

Analysis revealed three behaviour-based merchant archetypes along a business maturity journey: Novice Seller → Bold Expansionist → Master Merchant — each with distinct logistics needs, growth motivations, and pain points that the volume-based model had completely obscured.

Phase 2 — Quantitative Translation

I identified key behavioural metrics from the interview data that could operationalise each archetype in the company's database. Using two complementary analytical methods, I generated a lead list of over 3,000 expansion-stage merchants — the segment I had identified as the correct target for Product X, which directly contradicted the product team's assumptions about their ideal customer.

Impact

The research led to a full product feature revamp for Product X, targeting the right user group. The sales leads I generated drove a 408% to 1,851% increase in sales across different SEA markets — directly attributable to aligning product features and GTM strategy with research-validated merchant archetypes.
